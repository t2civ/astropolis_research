The Moon's gradient is very low (~1–1.5 °C/km). Mercury's near-surface thermal cycling (>600 °C swing) is a unique engineering constraint not captured by depth alone.

**Mixed taxonomy.** The strata system mixes depth-based divisions (Earth Surface through Ultra Subsurface) with composition/geography-based divisions (terrae vs. maria, polar ice deposits). This is inevitable given the physics — the same depth on the Moon can access very different resources depending on whether you are in the highlands or a mare. For the simulation, this means some strata are alternatives (you are at either a terrae or maria site, not both) rather than a sequential depth stack.

**Mars subsurface hydrology.** The existence, depth, and salinity of Martian groundwater or brines remain poorly constrained. Radar data (MARSIS, SHARAD) have suggested possible liquid water at 1.5 km depth beneath the south polar layered deposits, but these results are debated. The Mars Subsurface and Deep Crust strata may or may not contain accessible water; this is a major uncertainty for any Mars resource simulation.

**Mars core size.** The InSight mission initially yielded a core radius of ~1,830 km (Stähler et al. 2021), but subsequent reanalysis (Khan et al. 2023, Irving et al. 2023) suggested that the seismic reflector may have been a basal molten silicate layer rather than the core-mantle boundary itself, implying a smaller, denser core (~1,650 km radius). I used ~1,700 km as a round intermediate value, but this number may continue to shift.

**Ceres and Mercury internal structure.** Both are constrained primarily by gravity field data and modeling rather than direct seismic measurement. Depth boundaries (Ceres outer shell thickness, Mercury mantle-core boundary) carry uncertainties of tens of km.

**Missing solar system bodies.** The document does not include Venus (thick atmosphere, extreme surface conditions, but potentially valuable atmospheric mining), the Martian moons Phobos and Deimos (small, accessible, potentially volatile-rich — Phobos in particular is a candidate early Mars-orbit resource depot), the Jovian moons (Europa's subsurface ocean, Ganymede and Callisto's ice-rock crusts), the Saturnian system (Titan's hydrocarbon lakes and thick atmosphere, Enceladus's water plumes), or trans-Neptunian objects. Any of these could be added later using the same framework.

**Asteroid taxonomy simplification.** C/S/M covers the three major spectral classes but the actual Tholen and Bus-DeMeo taxonomies have dozens of subtypes. The simulation may eventually want at least a few more classes (e.g., V-type basaltic asteroids like Vesta fragments, or X-type which encompasses M along with E and P subtypes with very different inferred compositions). Additionally, large differentiated asteroids like Vesta have crust/mantle/core structure and arguably should not be treated as "bulk."

**Time-dependent resource accessibility.** Some deposits are transient or depletable on relevant timescales. Polar volatile cold traps on the Moon and Mercury could be partially destroyed by large impacts or disturbed by extraction activity itself (sublimation). Mars ground ice stability depends on obliquity cycles. These temporal factors are not captured in a static strata list.

## Notes

2. Earth "continental" strata include the continental shelf, encompassing near-shore seafloor mining and offshore drilling operations.

## Notes

2. Earth "continental" strata include the continental shelf, encompassing near-shore seafloor mining and offshore drilling operations.

## Moon

*Mean radius 1,737 km. Surface gravity 1.62 m/s² (0.17 g). No atmosphere. Key challenges: vacuum, abrasive dust, thermal cycling (±150 °C).*

- **Regolith** — 0–10 m (2–8 m in maria, 5–15 m in terrae). Impact-generated fines and breccias. O₂ from ilmenite, metals, ³He from solar-wind implantation, sintered construction feedstock.
- **Polar Shaded Regolith** — Permanently shadowed craters near both poles, 0 to ~few m depth. H₂O ice, CO₂, NH₃, organics cold-trapped at 40–100 K. Key cislunar volatile source. Extraction in permanent darkness.

- **Core** — ~1,400–1,737 km. Iron-nickel, radius ~330 km, partially molten. Speculative planet harvest.

## Mars

*Mean radius 3,390 km. Surface gravity 3.72 m/s² (0.38 g). Thin CO₂ atmosphere, ~0.6 kPa mean surface pressure. Mean surface temperature ~−60 °C. Key challenges: low pressure, cold, dust storms, perchlorates, communication delay.*

*Mean radius 2,440 km. Surface gravity 3.7 m/s² (0.38 g). Tenuous exosphere only. Extreme thermal environment: ~430 °C dayside, ~−180 °C nightside. Solar flux ~6–10× Earth's. Enormous iron core.*

## Ceres

*Mean radius 473 km. Surface gravity 0.28 m/s² (0.03 g). No atmosphere. Partially differentiated ice-rock body; largest asteroid belt object. Abundant water ice and hydrated minerals.*

## Vesta

- **Crust** — ~few m to ~20 km. Basaltic (eucrite) upper crust over orthopyroxenite (diogenite) lower crust. Rich in pyroxene, plagioclase, Fe, Mg.
- **Mantle** — ~20–150 km. Olivine-dominated ultramafic. Possibly exposed in Rheasilvia basin.
- **Core** — ~150–263 km. Iron-nickel, radius ~110 km. Speculative extraction.

## Pallas

*Mean radius 256 km. Surface gravity ~0.21 m/s² (0.02 g). No atmosphere. B-type (carbonaceous). Density ~2.8 g/cm³ suggests partial differentiation. No spacecraft visit; internal structure poorly constrained. High orbital inclination (~34.8°) increases rendezvous ΔV.*

## Io

*Mean radius 1,822 km. Surface gravity 1.80 m/s² (0.18 g). Tenuous SO₂ atmosphere. Most volcanically active body in the solar system; tidally heated by Jupiter. Extreme radiation environment (innermost Galilean moon, deep in Jupiter's magnetosphere). Surface constantly resurfaced.*

- **Surface** — 0–few m. Sulfur, SO₂ frost, silicate volcanic deposits. Rapidly overturned by eruptions.

- **Mantle** — ~30–1,200 km. Silicate, extensively partially molten. Global partial melt layer likely at ~30–50 km depth. Speculative extraction.
- **Core** — ~1,200–1,822 km. Iron or iron-sulfide, radius ~350–950 km (poorly constrained). Speculative planet harvest.

## Europa

*Mean radius 1,561 km. Surface gravity 1.31 m/s² (0.13 g). Thin O₂ exosphere. Global subsurface ocean beneath ice shell; strong astrobiological interest. Intense radiation environment. Key challenges: radiation shielding, ice penetration, planetary protection constraints.*

- **Ice Surface** — 0–few m. Radiation-processed water ice, hydrated salts (MgSO₄, NaCl), possible organics. Young, geologically active surface.
- **Ice Shell** — ~few m to ~15–25 km (estimates range 5–30 km). Water ice, fractures, possible brine pockets and sills. Convecting warm ice in lower portion.

- **Core** — ~1,000–1,561 km. Iron or iron-sulfide, radius ~350–650 km. Speculative planet harvest.

- **Core** — ~1,930–2,634 km. Iron or iron-sulfide, radius ~700 km. Generates intrinsic magnetic field. Speculative planet harvest.

- **Surface** — 0–few m. Ancient, heavily cratered ice-rock. Dark non-ice lag deposit.
- **Outer Shell** — ~few m to ~100 km. Ice-rock mixture, cold and rigid.
- **Possible Ocean** — ~100 to ~200 km depth. Thin liquid water layer if present; sustained by antifreeze solutes (NH₃, salts). Existence uncertain.

A few decisions worth flagging:

**Vesta and Pallas placement.** Vesta gets a full section with four strata — Dawn confirmed its differentiated structure and we have good constraints on crust, mantle, and core geometry. Pallas gets a short section with three coarse strata because its size (comparable to Ceres) warrants it, but no spacecraft has visited so the interior is largely inferred from density and spectral class. If Pallas turns out to be unimportant in the simulation, it could be demoted to the small bodies section.

**Galilean moon radiation.** I called out radiation intensity in each moon's header because it's a dominant engineering constraint that varies dramatically across the system — Io and Europa are deep inside Jupiter's magnetosphere, Ganymede has its own mini-magnetosphere providing partial shielding, and Callisto orbits outside the worst radiation belts. This matters for surface operations feasibility and equipment lifetime.

**Ganymede's inner ice barrier.** The high-pressure ice layer between the subsurface ocean and the silicate mantle is a structurally important detail — unlike Europa, where the ocean directly contacts the rocky seafloor, Ganymede's ocean is sealed between two ice layers. This affects both resource access modeling and any hypothetical deep extraction scenarios.

**Io's volcanism.** The constant resurfacing means Io's "surface" stratum is geologically distinct from any other body's — it's not an ancient regolith but a continuously renewed deposit. The magma ocean / extensive partial melt at shallow depth also means the crust–mantle boundary is unusually shallow and possibly gradational rather than sharp.

**Strata name prefixing.** With body names removed from strata items, the simulation's full identifier would be constructed as `{Section}/{Stratum}` (e.g., `Europa/Ice Shell`, `Earth/Continental Subsurface`). This keeps the document concise while allowing unambiguous programmatic reference.
