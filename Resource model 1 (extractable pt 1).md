# Nonvolatile Extractable Resources — Draft Proposal

## Approach

The goal is a set of roughly 10–15 nonvolatile resource categories that simultaneously serve three roles: (1) composition basis for describing any solid body or stratum by mass fraction, (2) tradeable commodities, and (3) inputs for downstream refining and manufacturing. To keep the count down, I use broad catchall categories for anything that constitutes a small mass fraction and has broadly similar extraction/trade characteristics, but I break out materials that are distinctive in distribution, extraction pathway, or economic importance.

Below I propose 12 items, then walk through how they cover solar system bodies and where the important simplifications lie.

---

## Proposed List (12 items)

**Bulk materials**

- **Stone** — Consolidated silicate rock: basalt, granite, gabbro, peridotite, feldspar-group minerals, olivine, pyroxene, quartz, and so on. This is the dominant mass component of planetary mantles and crusts, and the silicate fraction of stony asteroids. Also the primary feedstock for construction aggregate, glass, and (upon refining) silicon metal. Treat as the "everything else" bin for consolidated rock not captured by a more specific category.

- **Regolith** — Unconsolidated surface material: soil, sand, loose fragmental debris on airless bodies. Compositionally similar to underlying stone in many cases, but distinguished by ease of extraction (no drilling or blasting), direct utility for construction fill, radiation shielding, and sintering/3D-printing feedstock in space contexts. On Earth this also covers topsoil, alluvium, and sediments used in agriculture and construction.

**Iron group**

- **Iron** — Native metallic iron, typically alloyed with ~5–10% nickel (kamacite/taenite). The dominant material in planetary cores and M-type asteroids. This item does double duty as an extractable resource (native iron in metallic asteroids and core material) and as a refined resource (pig iron, direct-reduced iron). When refined from iron ores, the output is this same "iron" item, which simplifies the production chain.

- **Iron ores** — Oxide and hydroxide iron minerals as found in planetary crusts. Representative type: hematite (Fe₂O₃, ~70 wt% Fe). Includes magnetite, goethite, and similar minerals. Separated from the "industrial metal ores" catchall because iron is by far the most extracted and traded metal, and because the distinction between native iron and iron ores matters enormously for extraction economics (direct use vs. smelting).

**Metal ores**

- **Aluminum ores** — Aluminum-bearing ore minerals. On Earth the representative is bauxite (a mixture of gibbsite, boehmite, and diaspore; roughly 30–55 wt% Al₂O₃). On the Moon, anorthite (CaAl₂Si₂O₈, ~19 wt% Al) in anorthositic highlands is the relevant source. Separated out because aluminum is the second-most-produced metal and is the dominant metal in some crustal bodies (lunar highlands are ~25–30% anorthosite by mass).

- **Industrial metal ores** — Catchall for ores of copper, zinc, tin, lead, nickel, manganese, chromium, titanium, cobalt, tungsten, lithium, and similar metals. No single representative type; if forced, chalcopyrite (CuFeS₂) for copper or ilmenite (FeTiO₃) for titanium are reasonable stand-ins depending on context. This category absorbs a wide range of distinct commodities, which is the biggest abstraction cost.

- **Precious metal ores** — Gold, silver, and platinum-group metals (Pt, Pd, Rh, Ir, Ru, Os). Separated because of their extreme value-to-mass ratio, distinct distribution (concentrated in some asteroid types relative to terrestrial crust, where they were depleted into the core during differentiation), and unique role as monetary/store-of-value commodities. Representative: native gold or PGM-bearing sulfide concentrate.

- **Rare earth ores** — Ores of the lanthanides, yttrium, and scandium. Representative: bastnäsite ((Ce,La)CO₃F) or monazite ((Ce,La,Nd,Th)PO₄). Separated because of their strategic importance for magnets, catalysts, and electronics, and because their geochemistry and extraction are distinct from the industrial metals catchall (rare earths are geochemically coherent, concentrated in specific igneous and sedimentary settings, and require specialized hydrometallurgical processing).

**Energy and chemical feedstocks**

- **Radioactive ores** — Uranium and thorium minerals. Representative: uraninite (UO₂, ~88 wt% U). Small by mass fraction everywhere (parts per million in typical crust), but uniquely important for fission power. Thorium is included here since thorium fuel cycles are plausible in the simulation's future timeframe.

- **Carbonaceous material** — Solid carbon-rich material: coal and kerogen on Earth, carbonaceous chondrite matrix in C-type asteroids and comets. Representative for Earth: bituminous coal (~75 wt% C). Representative for asteroids: CI/CM chondrite matrix (~3–5 wt% C in a clay-mineral-and-organic matrix). Serves as an energy source (combustion on Earth), chemical feedstock (carbon for steel, polymers), and is a significant mass fraction of C-type asteroids and cometary dust.

**Other minerals**

- **Industrial minerals** — Catchall for non-metallic minerals of economic value: phosphate rock (apatite), limestone (calcite/dolomite for cement), potash (sylvite/carnallite for fertilizer), gypsum, salt (halite), fluorite, clays, and similar materials. Representative: apatite (Ca₅(PO₄)₃(F,Cl,OH)) given the critical role of phosphorus in agriculture. This is a heterogeneous group unified by being non-metallic, non-fuel, and industrially consumed.

- **Sulfur** — Native sulfur and sulfur-dominant compounds. ## Compositional Coverage

To verify completeness, here is how the list maps onto major body types and strata. All fractions are rough order-of-magnitude by mass and are presented to show that no large fraction is left unaccounted for; exact values would be defined during simulation setup.

**Earth's core** (~32% of Earth's mass): approximately 90–93% iron, 5–6% sulfur, with remaining percent or two absorbable by iron (dissolved O, Si, Ni are part of the alloy). This stratum is described almost entirely by two resource items.

**Earth's mantle** (~67%): effectively 100% stone. Iron, magnesium, silicon, and oxygen are chemically bound in silicate minerals (olivine, pyroxene, garnet, bridgmanite). There is no free iron or separate ore phase at mantle conditions — everything is silicate. The elemental composition of "stone" thus includes substantial iron (~8 wt% FeO), but as a constituent of silicate minerals rather than as a separate resource.

**Earth's continental crust** (~0.35%): predominantly stone (~93–96%), with iron ores (~2%), aluminum ores (~1%), industrial minerals (~0.5–1%), carbonaceous material (~0.2–0.5% counting coal measures and sedimentary organics), industrial metal ores (~0.3%), sulfur (~0.1%), and trace fractions of the remaining ores. Regolith makes up the uppermost few meters and is defined by the stratum model rather than bulk crust composition.

**Moon**: core is ~1–2% of lunar mass and predominantly iron. The mantle (~80%) is stone (olivine and pyroxene). The crust (~15–18%) is stone with a high aluminum ores component (anorthosite), so perhaps ~70% stone and ~25% aluminum ores at the crustal stratum. The surface stratum is regolith (up to ~10 m deep in highlands).

**S-type asteroid**: roughly 60–70% stone, 15–25% iron (metal phase), 3–5% sulfur (troilite, FeS), and a few percent attributable to industrial metal ores and precious metal ores. Surface is regolith.

**M-type asteroid**: roughly 80–90% iron, 5–10% sulfur, 2–5% stone (silicate inclusions), with small but potentially high-value fractions of precious metal ores and industrial metal ores (PGMs and Co/Ni). Surface is metallic regolith.

**C-type asteroid**: roughly 40–60% stone (hydrated silicates — phyllosilicates, serpentine), 5–15% carbonaceous material, 5–15% iron (both metallic and in silicates), a few percent sulfur, and the remainder in volatiles (water, organics) that belong on the volatile list. Surface is regolith.

**Cometary nucleus** (nonvolatile fraction only, ~30–40% of total mass): roughly 60–70% stone (silicate dust grains), 20–30% carbonaceous material, and small fractions of iron, sulfur, and industrial minerals. The volatile fraction (water ice, CO₂, CO, NH₃, CH₄) dominates by mass and will go on the separate list.

No body type leaves a large unaccounted mass fraction.

## Key Design Decisions and Trade-offs

The choice to separate iron into two items (native iron and iron ores) uses two slots but is justified by the fundamental difference in extraction chains and by iron's outsized importance — iron alone accounts for roughly a third of Earth's total mass and is the single most important structural metal. In a simulation spanning Earth-surface and asteroid mining, conflating hematite smelting with cutting chunks of metallic asteroid would obscure meaningfully different economics.

Nickel is folded into "iron" (native Fe-Ni alloy) for body composition and into "industrial metal ores" for terrestrial nickel-sulfide and nickel-laterite deposits. This avoids spending a slot on nickel specifically, at the cost of some ambiguity when a body has native Fe-Ni: the nickel content is implicit in the elemental composition of the "iron" resource.

The line between "stone" and "regolith" is physical form, not composition. In the simulation, these would typically appear in different strata (surface vs. subsurface) and have different extraction costs. Regolith is simply easier to move and process than competent rock.

"Carbonaceous material" groups terrestrial coal with asteroidal carbonaceous matrix even though they are geologically very different. The unifying thread is that both are solid, carbon-rich, and serve as energy or chemical feedstocks. This is one of the coarser abstractions in the list.

## Simplifications and Important Omissions

**Heterogeneity within catchall categories.** "Industrial metal ores" lumps together copper, titanium, lithium, manganese, and a dozen other metals with very different geochemistry, extraction methods, and market prices. If the simulation needs to model, say, lithium supply constraints for batteries or titanium for aerospace structures, this catchall will not resolve those dynamics. The same applies to "industrial minerals," which groups cement feedstocks with fertilizer minerals. If the simulation discovers that any particular metal or mineral is a critical bottleneck, it may need to be promoted to its own item.

**Ore grade and concentration are abstracted away.** In reality, the crustal abundance of copper (~60 ppm) is orders of magnitude below economic ore grade (~0.5–1% Cu). The simulation's stratum composition numbers are bulk averages; actual extraction requires ore deposits at much higher grades. The model will need some mechanism (even a simple "concentration factor" or "reserve fraction") to avoid implying that any rock can be mined for any metal at bulk-crust proportions.

**Hydration state.** Hydrated silicates (serpentine, clay minerals) are compositionally different from anhydrous silicates (olivine, pyroxene), and their water content is economically important in space contexts (water extraction from C-type asteroids). In this list, hydrated silicates are still "stone," and the bound water would need to be tracked as a volatile fraction embedded in stone for such bodies. This might require an annotation on the stone resource for hydrated variants, or a special rule for C-type asteroid strata.

**Oxygen is invisible.** Oxygen is the most abundant element by mass in the crust (~46 wt%) and the second most in the whole Earth (~30 wt%), but it never appears as a separate extractable resource because it is always chemically bound in other resources (silicates, oxides). In the simulation, oxygen as a refined resource would be produced by processing stone, iron ores, or water. This is physically correct but worth flagging so it is not accidentally omitted from the refining model.

**No separate "construction minerals" category.** Limestone (for cement), sand and gravel (for concrete aggregate), and dimension stone are each enormous commodity markets on Earth. They are split between "stone," "regolith," and "industrial minerals" in this list. If the simulation models construction in detail, this split may need attention.

**Semiconductor and photovoltaic feedstocks.** Silicon (from silica sand → stone), gallium and indium (from industrial metal ores), and germanium are all abstracted into their parent categories. If the simulation tracks solar panel or chip production specifically, these supply chains are hidden inside catchall terms.

# Splitting "Carbonaceous Material" — Coal, Organics, and Tholins

## Coal as a separate item

You're right to flag this. Coal and the organic fraction of carbonaceous chondrites differ in carbon content by more than an order of magnitude (~75–95 wt% C for bituminous-to-anthracite coal versus ~3–5 wt% C in CI chondrite bulk material), and they differ completely in distribution, extraction method, trade context, and end use. Coal is one of the largest commodity markets on Earth by tonnage (approximately 8 billion tonnes/year as of the early 2020s, per IEA), with dedicated extraction infrastructure, and it simply doesn't exist off-Earth. It earns its own slot.

**Kerogen** can be folded into "coal" with modest violence to reality. Kerogen is solid fossil organic matter disseminated in sedimentary rock — essentially immature coal or oil precursor depending on type. The important varieties for the simulation are Type III kerogen (terrestrial plant–derived, high C, yields gas and resembles coal thermally) and oil shale deposits (Type I/II kerogen, which upon retorting yields shale oil). In the real world, oil shale is surface-mined and retorted much like coal processing, and the extracted product would enter the refining chain as a liquid fuel (joining the volatiles list at that point). Calling the in-ground solid resource "coal" and letting the refining model sort out outputs (coke, syngas, shale oil) is a clean abstraction. The main thing lost is that kerogen-rich shale is really a few percent organic in a matrix of stone — but the same is true of coal measures, which include interbedded shales and sandstones that a coal mine produces as waste rock. The simulation can handle this through the elemental composition of "coal" not being 100% carbon.

## What happens to the asteroidal and cometary organics

Once coal is removed, the remaining non-coal carbon-rich solids in the solar system are a distinct resource in their own right. I'd propose calling this **"organics"** — short, intuitive, and chemically appropriate since we mean complex solid organic compounds. This category covers:

**Meteoritic insoluble organic matter (IOM):** The dominant organic phase in carbonaceous chondrites, a macromolecular kerogen-like polymer with approximate composition C₁₀₀H₇₀N₃O₁₂S₂ (Derenne & Robert 2010, *Meteoritics & Planetary Science* 45:1461–1475). This is ~3–5 wt% of CI/CM chondrites.

**Cometary CHON particles:** Complex organic grains observed by Giotto and Stardust in cometary dust. These constitute roughly 20–30% of the nonvolatile dust fraction of comets (Fomenkova et al. 1994, *Geochimica et Cosmochimica Acta* 58:4503–4512; Bardyn et al. 2017, *MNRAS* 469:S712–S722 for 67P/Churyumov-Gerasimenko results).

**Tholins** (discussed below).

The key point is that with coal removed, the "carbonaceous chondrite matrix" I previously treated as a single resource now gets decomposed into its mineral constituents across multiple categories. A CI chondrite body in the simulation would have a composition approximately like: 55–65% stone (phyllosilicates such as serpentine and saponite, plus minor carbonates — the hydrated silicate issue I flagged previously), 8–12% iron ores (magnetite and ferrihydrite), 5–8% sulfur (sulfides and sulfates), 3–5% organics, perhaps 1% iron (dispersed metallic grains), with the remainder being volatiles (water bound in hydrated minerals, plus free ice). This decomposition is actually more physically honest than lumping the whole matrix into one item, since the matrix is genuinely a mixture of mineralogically distinct phases. The cost is that "stone" now needs to do extra work representing hydrated silicates whose water content is economically valuable — but that cost already existed and was flagged.

## On tholins

This is an interesting idea and I don't think it's unreasonable, but I'd argue against a dedicated slot. Here's my reasoning.

Tholins are real and abundant in the right places. The term (coined by Sagan & Khare 1979, *Nature* 277:102–107) covers complex nitrogen-rich organic polymers produced by UV and charged-particle irradiation of simple C/N/H molecules (methane, nitrogen, ammonia). They are found or strongly inferred on Titan's surface (the equatorial dune fields alone may contain hundreds of thousands of cubic kilometers of tholin-like material per Lorenz et al. 2006, *Science* 312:724–727), on Pluto and Charon (the reddish-brown deposits, Grundy et al. 2016, *Science* 351:aad9189), on cometary surfaces (the dark crust of 67P), and broadly across Kuiper Belt objects and Centaurs. The nitrogen content is their most distinctive chemical feature — typically 5–15 wt% N depending on formation conditions — and nitrogen is genuinely scarce in the inner solar system's solid inventory.

However, the arguments against a separate slot are:

First, tholins are compositionally a subset of complex solid organics. They differ from meteoritic IOM and cometary CHON mainly in having higher nitrogen content and being produced by radiation chemistry rather than aqueous or thermal processing, but the end-use case is the same: carbon and nitrogen feedstock for chemical industry. The simulation's refining model can handle the difference in N yield through the elemental composition assigned to "organics" for outer-solar-system bodies (nitrogen-rich) versus inner-solar-system bodies (nitrogen-poor).

Second, tholins are poorly characterized as a material. Laboratory tholins vary enormously depending on gas mixture, energy source, and temperature. We don't have a ground-truth sample from Titan or Pluto, so defining a representative composition for the simulation would be quite speculative even by the standards of this project.

Third, the practical extraction and trade scenarios for tholins are extremely far-future. Titan is a compelling destination for many reasons, but tholin harvesting is not likely to be an early economic driver — the same body has methane and ethane lakes (volatiles) and a thick nitrogen atmosphere (volatile) that are more immediately useful. Tholins become interesting mainly as a carbon/nitrogen feedstock once you have industrial infrastructure in the outer solar system, at which point the distinction between tholin-derived and IOM-derived carbon is a refining detail rather than a resource classification issue.

My recommendation is to fold tholins into "organics" and handle the compositional variation through body-specific or stratum-specific elemental assignments. If the simulation later develops detailed outer-solar-system economics and the distinction between nitrogen-rich and nitrogen-poor organics turns out to be a critical bottleneck, tholins can be promoted to their own slot at that time. This is the same "promote when needed" logic I suggested for individual industrial metals.

# Volatile Extractable Resources

## Survey approach

I went through every body in the solar system that hosts a significant volatile reservoir — planetary and lunar atmospheres, surface ices and liquids, cometary compositions, and geological volatile deposits — and identified every molecular species that constitutes more than roughly 1% by mass or volume of at least one such reservoir. I then applied the secondary filter you described: species that fall below the 1% threshold but have outsized importance as commodities or industrial/engineering inputs. The result is 11 items.

## Body-by-body inventory

This is the empirical foundation for the list, so I'll walk through it fairly carefully.

**Venus.** The atmosphere (~4.8 × 10²⁰ kg, about 93× Earth's by mass) is 96.5% CO₂ and 3.5% N₂ by volume. Everything else is trace: SO₂ at ~150 ppm, argon at ~70 ppm, water vapor at 20–100 ppm. The significant volatiles are CO₂ and N₂. The nitrogen inventory is striking — Venus's atmospheric N₂ mass (~1.7 × 10¹⁹ kg) is roughly four times Earth's (~4.0 × 10¹⁸ kg). This matters for any scenario involving nitrogen supply in the inner solar system.

**Earth.** The atmosphere (5.15 × 10¹⁸ kg) gives us N₂ (78.1%), O₂ (20.9%), and Ar (0.93%), plus trace CO₂, neon, helium, methane, and krypton. The hydrosphere adds ~1.4 × 10²¹ kg of water. Geological volatiles include oil (conventional reserves roughly 1.7 × 10¹² barrels or ~2.3 × 10¹¹ tonnes; total petroleum system including unconventional resources considerably larger) and natural gas (predominantly methane, ~190 trillion cubic meters proved reserves per BP/IEA data as of mid-2020s). Natural gas folds into methane per your suggestion. Earth's atmospheric argon (~4.8 × 10¹⁶ kg) is the largest accessible argon reservoir in the solar system.

**Mars.** The atmosphere (~2.5 × 10¹⁶ kg) is 95.3% CO₂, 2.7% N₂, and 1.6% Ar, with minor O₂ (0.15%) and CO (0.075%) (Mahaffy et al. 2013, *Science* 341:263–266, from Curiosity's SAM instrument). The polar caps contain both water ice (the residual north polar cap, plus subsurface ice mapped by SHARAD and MARSIS radar) and seasonal CO₂ ice. Subsurface water ice at mid-latitudes has been confirmed by Phoenix and inferred broadly from neutron spectrometer data (Feldman et al. 2004, *JGR* 109:E09006). Mars's argon fraction (1.6%, or ~4 × 10¹⁴ kg) is the highest of any planetary atmosphere, and argon would be an unavoidable byproduct of any atmospheric processing for CO₂ or N₂.

**Jupiter and Saturn.** These are overwhelmingly hydrogen and helium. Jupiter's mass fractions are approximately 73% H₂ and 24% He (with number fractions of ~86% H₂ and ~13.6% He). Saturn is similar but more hydrogen-rich (~96% H₂ by number, ~3.25% He). Minor but absolutely enormous reservoirs include methane (~0.2–0.3% by mass, translating to ~4 × 10²⁴ kg for Jupiter alone), ammonia (~0.05% by mass, ~10²³ kg for Jupiter), water (uncertain but possibly 0.5–1% deep in the atmosphere), and hydrogen sulfide. The helium fraction is notable: Jupiter and Saturn together hold roughly 5 × 10²⁶ kg of He, including ~7 × 10²¹ kg of He-3 at the natural isotopic ratio of ~1.5 × 10⁻⁴ (Mahaffy et al. 2000, *JGR* 105:15061–15071, from Galileo probe mass spectrometer). These quantities are essentially infinite by any industrial standard but are trapped in deep gravity wells.

**Uranus and Neptune.** The key difference from the gas giants is enriched methane: 2.3% of Uranus's upper atmosphere and ~1.5% of Neptune's by volume (Lindal et al. 1987, *JGR* 92:14987–15001; Conrath et al. 1991, *JGR* 96:18907–18919). Their interiors are thought to contain massive amounts of water, ammonia, and methane in compressed "icy" phases (the term "ice giant" reflects this composition). Hydrogen (~80–83%) and helium (~15–19%) still dominate the observable atmosphere.

**Titan.** The atmosphere (~9.1 × 10¹⁸ kg, about 1.8× Earth's atmospheric mass despite Titan's much smaller size and lower gravity) is 98.4% N₂ and ~1.4% CH₄, with ~0.1% H₂. Titan's atmospheric nitrogen inventory (~9.0 × 10¹⁸ kg) is roughly 2.2 times Earth's atmospheric nitrogen — making Titan the largest accessible nitrogen reservoir in the solar system outside Earth and Venus. The surface hosts methane-ethane lakes and seas: Ligeia Mare alone is estimated at ~9,000 km³ (Mastrogiuseppe et al. 2014, *GRL* 41:1432–1437), and Kraken Mare is likely larger. Total surface liquid hydrocarbon volume is uncertain but probably tens of thousands of cubic kilometers, predominantly methane and ethane. Ethane (C₂H₆) is a significant fraction of the lake composition — Ontario Lacus appears ethane-enriched (Brown et al. 2008, *Nature* 454:607–610). I'll fold ethane into the methane category as a light hydrocarbon.

**Io.** The tenuous atmosphere and extensive surface frost deposits are dominated by SO₂, with sulfur vapor (S₂) in volcanic plumes. This is the only body where SO₂ is the dominant volatile. However, processing SO₂ yields elemental sulfur and oxygen, both already covered by other resource categories (solid sulfur and volatile oxygen). I'll argue below that Io's SO₂ does not need its own slot.

**Icy moons (Europa, Enceladus, Ganymede, Callisto, etc.).** These are predominantly water ice with varying amounts of non-ice components. The best-characterized volatile inventory comes from Enceladus's plumes: ~90–95% H₂O, ~3–5% CO₂, ~1% NH₃, ~0.5–1% CH₄, ~0.5–1% H₂, plus trace H₂S and organics (Waite et al. 2009, *Nature* 460:487–490; Waite et al. 2017, *Science* 356:155–159). Ammonia at ~1% is notable — it's one of the few bodies where NH₃ is confirmed at the percent level in a volatile reservoir.

**Comets.** The volatile composition of comets is well-characterized from ground-based and spacecraft observations. Using the compilation from Mumma & Charnley (2011, *ARAA* 49:471–524) and updated by Rosetta results from 67P/Churyumov-Gerasimenko, typical abundances relative to H₂O = 100% are: CO₂ at 5–15%, CO at 2–15% (highly variable, reaching 20–23% in Hale-Bopp per DiSanti & Mumma 2008), CH₃OH (methanol) at 1–3%, CH₄ at 0.5–1.6%, NH₃ at 0.5–1.4%, C₂H₆ at 0.1–2%, H₂S at 0.1–1.5%, and HCN at 0.1–0.25%. Rosetta also detected molecular O₂ at 1–10% relative to water in 67P (Bieler et al. 2015, *Nature* 526:678–681), which was one of the most unexpected results of that mission. CO and CO₂ are each individually at the multi-percent level, making both significant cometary volatiles.

**Pluto and Triton.** Surface ices are dominated by N₂ (>95% of volatile ice), with CH₄ at 1–2% and CO at ~0.5–1% (Grundy et al. 2016, *Science* 351:aad9189, for Pluto; Cruikshank et al. 1993 for Triton). Water ice forms the bedrock substrate beneath the volatile ices. Pluto's thin atmosphere (~1 Pa surface pressure) is predominantly N₂ with trace CH₄ and CO.

**Kuiper Belt objects and Centaurs.** Surfaces show varying mixtures of water ice, methane ice, nitrogen ice, and ammonia hydrate, depending on size, heliocentric distance, and thermal history. Large KBOs (Eris, Makemake, Haumea, Quaoar, Sedna) have surface spectra showing CH₄, N₂, and/or H₂O ice. Smaller objects are generally water ice–dominated.

**C-type asteroids.** As discussed previously, water is bound in hydrated silicates at 10–20 wt% extractable. This is the main volatile resource from asteroids. No significant free volatile species beyond this.

**Lunar polar deposits.** Water ice in permanently shadowed craters at the poles, confirmed by LCROSS (Colaprete et al. 2010, *Science* 330:463–468) and characterized by multiple orbital instruments. Estimated mass uncertain but possibly 600 million tonnes in the south polar region (Li et al. 2018, *PNAS* 115:8907–8912). Associated volatiles detected by LCROSS include H₂S, NH₃, SO₂, C₂H₄, CO₂, and CH₃OH at much lower concentrations.

## Proposed list (11 items)

**1. Water (H₂O).** The most widely distributed and arguably most important volatile resource in the solar system. Water serves as the feedstock for propellant production via electrolysis (yielding H₂ and O₂ for chemical propulsion), as radiation shielding, as life support consumable, as agricultural input, and as industrial solvent and coolant. It is found as: Earth's hydrosphere (1.4 × 10²¹ kg); Mars polar and subsurface ice (the north polar cap alone contains ~8.2 × 10¹⁷ kg per Zuber et al. 1998); lunar polar cold traps; hydrated minerals in C-type asteroids (10–20 wt% extractable); the dominant volatile in comets (~80% of volatile fraction); the bulk composition of icy moon shells and interiors (Europa's ice shell + ocean mass is roughly 3 × 10²¹ kg); and surface and subsurface ice on most outer solar system bodies. The "significant fraction" criterion is met overwhelmingly — water is >50% by mass of several solar system bodies and >80% of cometary volatiles.

**2. Hydrogen (H₂).** The dominant molecular species in gas giant and ice giant atmospheres: 73% of Jupiter by mass, ~71% of Saturn, ~63% of Uranus, ~62% of Neptune (these mass fractions differ from the commonly quoted volume fractions because helium is heavier per molecule). Hydrogen is the most abundant element in the solar system by a wide margin. Industrial and engineering significance includes: LH₂ as rocket propellant (specific impulse of LH₂/LOX is the highest of practical chemical propellants at ~450 s); feedstock for ammonia synthesis (Haber-Bosch), methanol synthesis, hydrogenation of organics, and Fischer-Tropsch production of liquid fuels; deuterium (D/H ~1.56 × 10⁻⁴ in Earth's ocean, somewhat lower in gas giants) as fusion fuel; and reducing agent in metallurgy. In the inner solar system, hydrogen is produced from water electrolysis. In the outer solar system, it is present in staggering quantity but trapped in deep gravity wells. Atmospheric mining of gas giants has been proposed (Bryan Palaszewski, various NASA technical reports, 1990s–2010s) but remains far-future.

**3. Helium (He).** The second most abundant gas giant volatile: 24% of Jupiter by mass, ~25% of Saturn, ~26% of Uranus, ~32% of Neptune. On Earth, helium is extracted from certain natural gas deposits (up to ~7% He in some fields in the US, Qatar, and Algeria), with global production around 160 million m³/year. Industrial uses include: cryogenic coolant (essential for MRI magnets, superconducting systems, and rocket propellant handling); pressurant gas for rocket fuel tanks; inert atmosphere for welding and semiconductor manufacturing; and leak detection. He-3 (~15 ppm of natural helium in gas giants, ~10–20 ppb implanted in lunar regolith by solar wind) is a potential fuel for aneutronic D-He³ fusion, which would produce charged particles rather than neutrons, enabling direct energy conversion. The He-3 question has received extensive attention (Wittenberg, Santarius & Kulcinski 1986, *Fusion Technology* 10:167–178; Schmitt 2006, *Return to the Moon*) and represents one of the speculative but high-value economic drivers for both lunar surface operations and eventual gas giant resource extraction.

**4. Nitrogen (N₂).** Major atmospheric component on Earth (78%, 4.0 × 10¹⁸ kg), Titan (98.4%, ~9.0 × 10¹⁸ kg), Venus (3.5%, ~1.7 × 10¹⁹ kg), and Mars (2.7%, ~6.8 × 10¹⁴ kg). Present as surface ice on Pluto (dominant volatile ice) and Triton. Nitrogen is essential for life support as a buffer gas in breathing atmospheres (preventing oxygen toxicity and fire risk), for agriculture (nitrogen fixation into fertilizer accounts for roughly half of global food production per Erisman et al. 2008, *Nature Geoscience* 1:636–639), and for chemical industry (ammonia, nitric acid, explosives, polymers). Nitrogen is notably scarce in the inner solar system outside Earth — Mars's total atmospheric N₂ (~6.8 × 10¹⁴ kg) is roughly 0.02% of Earth's, creating a potential bottleneck for Mars agriculture and terraforming scenarios. This scarcity gives nitrogen strategic importance disproportionate to its mass.

**5. Oxygen (O₂).** Free molecular oxygen constitutes 20.95% of Earth's atmosphere (1.08 × 10¹⁸ kg), the only body with a substantial O₂ atmosphere (a product of photosynthesis over ~2.4 billion years). Oxygen was also detected at 1–10% relative to water in comet 67P's coma, believed to be primordial O₂ trapped in ice rather than photolytic product (Bieler et al. 2015). Everywhere else, oxygen must be produced: from water electrolysis (2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂), from CO₂ reduction (the MOXIE instrument on Perseverance demonstrated CO₂ → CO + ½O₂ on Mars; Hecht et al. 2021, *Science Advances* 7:eabh3099), or from regolith processing (molten oxide electrolysis, ilmenite reduction, etc.). Oxygen is the most massive component of silicate minerals (~40–45 wt%), so it is chemically abundant in rocky bodies but energetically costly to extract from oxides. As a volatile resource, oxygen is critical as: LOX rocket oxidizer (by mass, LOX is the majority of most propellant loads — the LH₂/LOX mixture ratio is ~6:1 by mass, and LOX/CH₄ is ~3.5:1); life support consumable; and industrial oxidizer. Despite being primarily a *produced* resource off-Earth, it warrants its own slot because of its massive demand in propulsion and life support, its abundance on Earth, and its confirmed presence in cometary ices.

**6. Carbon dioxide (CO₂).** Dominant atmospheric volatile on Venus (96.5%, ~4.6 × 10²⁰ kg) and Mars (95.3%, ~2.4 × 10¹⁶ kg). Present in comets at 5–15% of total volatiles. CO₂ ice forms Mars's seasonal polar caps. CO₂ is the key ISRU feedstock on Mars: the Sabatier reaction (CO₂ + 4H₂ → CH₄ + 2H₂O) produces both propellant (methane) and water from atmospheric CO₂ and imported or local hydrogen, and direct electrolysis or solid oxide electrolysis can split CO₂ into CO and O₂. On Earth, CO₂ is used in food and beverage carbonation, enhanced oil recovery, chemical synthesis (urea, methanol, carbonates), and as a supercritical solvent. Venus's atmosphere contains an almost incomprehensibly large CO₂ reservoir — roughly 2,000× Mars's atmospheric CO₂ — though Venus's deep gravity well and extreme surface conditions make access far harder.

**7. Carbon monoxide (CO).** This is the most debatable item on the list, so I'll present the case in some detail. CO constitutes 2–15% of cometary volatiles, reaching 20–23% in some well-studied comets (Hale-Bopp: Disanti & Mumma 2008, *ApJ* 680:1–19). CO ice is present on Pluto's surface at ~0.5–1% of the volatile ice layer and on Triton at similar levels. It is a trace atmospheric component on Mars (0.075%) and Venus (~17 ppm). CO has distinct industrial uses: it is the essential component of synthesis gas (CO + H₂) for Fischer-Tropsch production of liquid hydrocarbons; it is the primary reducing agent in iron smelting (blast furnace: Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂); and it is a feedstock for methanol synthesis (CO + 2H₂ → CH₃OH) and other chemical processes. The case for separating CO from CO₂ rests on three points. First, it meets the "significant fraction" criterion independently through cometary abundances. Second, its physical properties are radically different from CO₂ — boiling point –191.5°C versus –78.5°C — placing it in a different volatility class. CO ice is stable at temperatures where CO₂ has long since sublimated, affecting which outer solar system bodies retain CO versus CO₂. Third, its industrial chemistry is distinct: CO is a reducing agent and syngas component, while CO₂ is an oxidized end-product. The case for merging rests on the relative ease of interconversion (water-gas shift reaction: CO + H₂O ⇌ CO₂ + H₂), meaning the two are practically fungible in many industrial contexts. I favor separation but recognize this is a judgment call.

**8. Methane (CH₄).** A significant atmospheric component of Uranus (2.3%), Neptune (1.5%), and Titan (1.4%). Present in cometary ices at 0.5–1.6% of total volatiles. On Titan, methane participates in a hydrological cycle analogous to Earth's water cycle — methane rain, methane rivers, and methane-ethane lakes and seas containing tens of thousands of cubic kilometers of liquid hydrocarbons. On Earth, methane is the primary component of natural gas (~95%), a massive energy commodity (~4 trillion m³/year global production). This category subsumes natural gas, ethane (C₂H₆, a significant component of Titan's lakes), and other light hydrocarbons (propane, butane, acetylene) as minor constituents. Engineering significance includes: methalox propulsion (LOX/CH₄, used in SpaceX Raptor, Blue Origin BE-4, and numerous other next-generation engines); feedstock for hydrogen production (steam methane reforming: CH₄ + H₂O → CO + 3H₂); polymer and chemical feedstock; and ISRU product on Mars via Sabatier reaction. The choice of methane over hydrogen as a rocket fuel for Mars return missions (Zubrin & Baker 1992, the "Mars Direct" architecture) rests on methane's higher density, storability, and producibility from Martian CO₂.

**9. Ammonia (NH₃).** This item does not clear the "significant fraction" threshold as cleanly as the others — it is ~1% of Enceladus plume composition, ~0.5–1.4% of cometary volatiles, and ~0.03% of Jupiter's and Saturn's upper tropospheric composition by volume. Its inclusion is justified primarily by the "special consideration" pathway: industrial importance and role as a nitrogen carrier. On Earth, ammonia production via the Haber-Bosch process (~180 Mt/year) is one of the largest-volume chemical syntheses in human industry, consuming ~1–2% of global energy production. Virtually all synthetic nitrogen fertilizer derives from ammonia, making it foundational to modern agriculture. In the outer solar system, ammonia is thought to be a significant component of icy moon interiors — ammonia-water eutectic melting at –97°C (compared to 0°C for pure water) is the standard explanation for why subsurface oceans can persist on moons that would otherwise be frozen solid (Kargel 1992, *Icarus* 100:556–574). Ammonia is also a candidate fuel (direct combustion or fuel cell), a refrigerant, and potentially a hydrogen carrier for energy transport. Its role as the primary nitrogen-bearing molecule in the outer solar system's ice inventory gives it strategic significance for any scenario involving food production beyond Earth.

**10. Argon (Ar).** This is the weakest item on the list, included because it passes the "significant fraction" criterion for Mars (1.6% of the atmosphere, the second-largest component after CO₂) and nearly passes for Earth (0.93%). Argon is the most abundant noble gas on both planets and the cheapest noble gas industrially (~$2–5/kg on Earth versus ~$5,000/kg for xenon). It is an unavoidable byproduct of atmospheric processing on both Earth and Mars — any facility that extracts CO₂ or N₂ from Mars's atmosphere will accumulate argon. Uses include: inert shield gas for welding and additive manufacturing (critical for in-space manufacturing in pressurized habitats); inert atmosphere for semiconductor and materials processing; thermal insulation (argon-filled windows and panels); and potential ion thruster propellant (lower performance than xenon but vastly cheaper and more available; Massey et al. 2008, *AIAA* 2008-4824). If the simulation models Mars ISRU with any fidelity, argon will appear as a coproduct whether or not it has its own resource slot — giving it a slot allows tracking its production and potential trade value rather than discarding it as waste.

**11. Oil (petroleum).** Earth-specific liquid fossil hydrocarbons, as you've already accepted. The largest commodity market on Earth by value (~$2–3 trillion/year at typical mid-2020s prices). Composition is a complex mixture of alkanes, cycloalkanes, and aromatics ranging from C₅ to C₄₀+. Distinguished from coal (solid fossil carbon) by phase, composition, extraction method, and end use. Distinguished from natural gas/methane by molecular weight range and phase. In the simulation, oil would be extracted from Earth's crust, refined into fuels (gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, rocket-grade RP-1 kerosene) and petrochemical feedstocks (ethylene, propylene, aromatics for plastics, synthetic fibers, pharmaceuticals). Oil's role diminishes as the simulation extends beyond Earth — it has no off-world analog — but it would be a major early-game trade commodity for any Earth-based space economy, particularly for RP-1 kerosene as rocket fuel.

## Mergers and exclusions

**Natural gas → methane.** Per your suggestion, and straightforward. Natural gas is ~95% CH₄ with minor C₂H₆, C₃H₈, and C₄H₁₀. The methane category absorbs these.

**Ethane and light hydrocarbons → methane.** Ethane is a significant fraction of Titan's lake composition and is distinct from methane in several ways (it is liquid at Titan's surface temperature while methane is near its triple point and can evaporate). However, the two are co-located on every body where either is found, they are chemically similar (differing by one carbon), and they serve similar industrial purposes (fuel, chemical feedstock). Separating ethane would create an item with essentially one relevant body (Titan). The simulation can track ethane-enriched versus methane-dominated hydrocarbon deposits through body-specific compositions within the methane category.

**Sulfur dioxide (SO₂) → not a separate volatile.** SO₂ is the dominant volatile on Io, but it decomposes to elemental sulfur and oxygen, both already covered. Io's sulfur deposits are handled under the solid sulfur resource; the oxygen liberated from SO₂ processing goes to O₂. Including SO₂ as a separate volatile would create an item that exists on essentially one body and whose end products are already tracked. The simplification cost is that the simulation won't distinguish between mining elemental sulfur and processing SO₂ frost on Io, which is an extraction-method detail rather than a resource classification issue.

**Hydrogen sulfide (H₂S) → excluded.** Present at 0.1–1.5% of cometary volatiles and in gas giant deep atmospheres, but never a dominant volatile anywhere. It would serve primarily as a sulfur source, duplicating the solid sulfur resource. Borderline by the "significant fraction" criterion (approaching 1% in some comets and in Enceladus plumes at ~1–2%), but its industrial role (Claus process to recover sulfur, or simply as an undesirable contaminant in natural gas processing) doesn't justify a separate slot.

**Methanol (CH₃OH) → excluded.** Present at 1–3% of cometary volatiles, which technically meets the "significant fraction" criterion for comets. However, methanol is a one-carbon alcohol that is industrially synthesized from CO + 2H₂ (syngas) and serves similar end uses to methane-derived products. In the simulation, cometary methanol would be captured alongside other volatiles during processing and either used directly or reformed. It does not have a distinct enough extraction, trade, or use profile to justify a slot. This is the strongest exclusion case — methanol has a legitimate claim by the abundance criterion — and I would not object if you chose to include it.

**Deuterium and He-3 → tracked as derived products, not separate resources.** Deuterium (D) is an isotope of hydrogen present at D/H ~1.56 × 10⁻⁴ in Earth's oceans, with somewhat lower ratios in gas giants and higher ratios in Venus's atmosphere and some cometary water (~3 × 10⁻⁴). He-3 is ~15 ppm of natural helium. Both are fusion fuels of potentially enormous value. However, they are extracted by isotope separation from hydrogen/water and helium respectively, not mined as distinct resources. The simulation should track them as outputs of hydrogen and helium processing rather than as in-ground resources. This parallels how refined metals (steel, aluminum) would be tracked as outputs of ore processing.

**Xenon (Xe) → excluded.** Xenon is the propellant of choice for current ion thrusters (Dawn, Starlink Gen 1) and has properties (high atomic mass, low ionization energy) that make it excellent for this purpose. However, it is present at only ~87 ppb in Earth's atmosphere and comparable trace levels everywhere else — it is never a "significant fraction" of any body. Global production is ~50–60 tonnes/year. In the simulation, xenon could be tracked as a trace byproduct of atmospheric processing (like the rare earths of the volatile world), but it doesn't warrant a dedicated slot. SpaceX has already moved Starlink to krypton thrusters, and argon-based systems are under development, suggesting the space economy may move away from xenon dependence.

## Interaction with the solid resource list

Several resources bridge the solid and volatile lists, and the simulation will need to handle these crossovers in its extraction and refining model.

Water appears both as free ice/liquid (volatile resource) and as chemically bound hydroxyl in hydrated silicates (component of solid "stone"). Processing C-type asteroid stone releases water as a volatile product. The simulation should distinguish between mining free water ice (e.g., on Mars or the Moon) and dehydrating hydrated minerals (e.g., on Ceres or C-type asteroids) — different extraction processes with different energy costs, but the same output.

Oxygen is present as a volatile (Earth's atmosphere, cometary O₂) and as the dominant anion in all silicate minerals (~40–45 wt% of stone). Extracting oxygen from regolith via molten oxide electrolysis or ilmenite reduction is a fundamentally different process from atmospheric separation or water electrolysis and should be modeled differently, but the product enters the same oxygen inventory.

Sulfur appears as a solid resource (native sulfur, sulfide and sulfate minerals) and in volatile forms (SO₂ on Io, H₂S in comets and gas giants). I've excluded volatile sulfur compounds from the volatile list to avoid double-counting, but the simulation should recognize that some sulfur extraction involves processing gaseous or frozen sulfur compounds rather than mining solid deposits.

Methane on Earth exists in geological reservoirs (natural gas) that are extracted by drilling, similar to oil. On Titan, methane is an atmospheric and surface liquid resource. On Uranus and Neptune, it is an atmospheric trace component. These are very different extraction scenarios unified by the same molecular species.

## Usage Notes

The stone and regolith references reflect two ends of silicate differentiation: stone is the Mg-rich ultramafic composition that dominates planetary mantles by mass, while regolith is the Ca-Al-Fe-enriched basaltic composition typical of crustal surfaces. Both compositions are body- and layer-specific in practice; these references are solar-system-weighted defaults. Earth's continental crust (granitic, ~72% SiO₂) differs substantially from either reference and would need a per-layer override.

Chemically bound water — as in C-type asteroid phyllosilicates at 10–20 wt% — should be assigned to the water resource category even though extraction requires thermal processing of what is physically a silicate rock. The energy cost and technology of extraction are separate parameters from the resource classification.

Precious metals at ~400 ppm total constitute <0.05% of their ore's mass but dominate its value. The simulation will need to handle value-per-tonne spanning roughly six orders of magnitude across the 27 resource types (from stone at ~\$0/t to precious metal ores valued for their trace PGM content at effectively ~\$10⁵–10⁶/t of contained metal).

## Notes

**The hydrothermal gap.** The dominant systemic difference from terrestrial ores is the near-total absence of hydrothermal concentration. On Earth, water circulating through crust at tectonic boundaries concentrated Cu, Zn, Pb, Sn, W, Mo, Ag, Au, Bi, Sb, and many other elements by factors of 10³–10⁵ above crustal average. Off-Earth, only Mars shows evidence of ancient hydrothermal activity, and it was brief and localized compared to Earth's continuous 4-billion-year recycling. This removes from availability an entire class of metals — essentially the chalcophile suite plus certain lithophile elements like W and Sn — that defined terrestrial industrial metallurgy from the Bronze Age onward. Off-Earth technology must be built around Cr-Ti-Ni-Co-Mn alloys and ceramics rather than Cu-Zn-Pb-Sn alloys and solders.

**The volatile gradient.** Resource availability follows a steep gradient with solar distance. The inner solar system (Moon, Mercury, near-Earth asteroids) is refractory-rich but volatile-poor: abundant Fe, Ti, Al, Cr, PGMs, but negligible H₂O, C, N, S. The outer solar system (Ceres, Jovian/Saturnian moons) is volatile-rich but metal-poor: abundant ice, organics, nitrogen, but low metal concentrations in undifferentiated ice-rock mixtures. A functioning off-Earth economy requires supply chains spanning this gradient, importing volatiles sunward and metals outward. This is the spatial resource-complementarity that will define interplanetary trade.

**Cross-category coupling.** Three sets of categories are coupled at the extraction level and cannot be independently scaled. Third, lunar ilmenite mining co-produces iron and titanium — the iron ore and a significant fraction of the industrial metal supply share a single source.

**Strategic gaps.** Several elements that are routine in terrestrial industry have no concentrated off-Earth source and would need to be either imported from Earth, substituted, or done without. ## Cross-cutting notes

**Coupled extraction streams.** Several categories share extraction operations and cannot be independently scaled. Lunar KREEP mining produces rare earth ore, the phosphate fraction of industrial minerals, and the only significant off-Earth Th and U — a single operation feeding three categories. **The chalcophile deficit.** The blended industrial metal ores entry understates the problem. **Byproduct value inversion.** Several elements undergo dramatic value shifts between Earth and off-Earth economies. The two halves of this blend are chemically distinct in ways that matter for processing. Earth kerogen is hydrogen-rich: the atomic H/C ratio is ~1.4, reflecting its origin as thermally immature Type I–II material (lacustrine algal and marine planktonic) that retains most of its original aliphatic chains. This is feedstock that can be retorted or hydrocracked into synthetic crude with relatively modest hydrogen input. Off-Earth organics are hydrogen-poor: atomic H/C drops to ~0.7–1.0 across the weighted mix of asteroidal IOM (H/C ~0.7), cometary organics (H/C ~1.0), and Titan tholins (H/C ~1.2). The IOM that dominates by mass is a heavily cross-linked aromatic macromolecule — structurally closer to anthracite than to oil shale — and requires either pyrolysis (yielding light gases plus a carbon-rich residue) or catalytic hydrogenation (consuming scarce off-Earth hydrogen) to produce useful hydrocarbons. N at 4.5% is the strategically critical feature and the primary reason this category exists as a distinct resource stream. Earth kerogen contributes only ~2.3% N; the off-Earth fraction contributes ~6.6%, driven largely by Titan tholins (~22% N, predominantly in nitrile and amine functional groups) and cometary CHON (~6% N). In the off-Earth economy, organics/tholins may be mined primarily for nitrogen recovery, with the carbon and hydrogen treated as co-products. S at 3.6% is distributed across both fractions — Earth kerogen carries ~2% S (in thiophenic rings and sulfide bridges) and off-Earth IOM carries ~5.3% S (in troilite inclusions and organic sulfides). This sulfur is chemically bound and released during thermal processing; it can be captured as elemental sulfur or sulfuric acid. The off-Earth fraction weighting assumes C-type asteroid IOM at 60%, cometary organics at 25%, and Titan tholins at 15% by mass — reflecting accessibility rather than total inventory. Titan's tholin deposits are enormous (estimated 10⁸–10⁹ tonnes on the surface alone) but lie deep in Saturn's gravity well; C-type near-Earth asteroids are far more accessible per unit of delta-v. **Off-Earth-valued byproducts:** N (binding constraint on agriculture and chemical industry — this stream and the atmosphere of Titan are the only concentrated nitrogen sources beyond Earth and Mars), S (chemical feedstock), deuterium (off-Earth IOM and cometary organics carry D/H ratios 2–10× terrestrial, making them an enriched deuterium source for potential fusion applications). **Earth-valued byproducts:** V and Ni (recovered from kerogen retorting residues — vanadium from oil shale retort is already a commercial source on Earth, and kerogen-hosted V at 150 ppm in the blend exceeds the grade of many vanadium ore deposits), Mo (chelated in organic complexes, recoverable from ash).
